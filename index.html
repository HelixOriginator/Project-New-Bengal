Supreme Court Judgment — 03 April 2025

2025 INSC 437 · Civil Appeal out of SLP(C) No. 9586 of 2024 & Ors. · Bench: CJI Sanjiv Khanna & J. Sanjay Kumar

Supreme Court of India CBI Investigation ED / PMLA 2016–2025

WBSSC Recruitment Scam — En Bloc Cancellation Upheld

The Supreme Court upheld the Calcutta High Court's judgment of 22 April 2024, affirming cancellation of the entire 2016 selection process by the West Bengal Central School Service Commission (WBSSC) for approximately 24,640 posts — Assistant Teachers (Classes IX–XII) and Non-Teaching Staff (Groups C & D). The ruling rejected appeals by the State of West Bengal, WBSSC, and affected selectees.

"Manipulations and frauds on a large scale, coupled with the attempted cover-up, have dented the selection process beyond repair and partial redemption. The credibility and legitimacy of the selection are denuded." — Para 20, 2025 INSC 437, Supreme Court of India

WBSSC admitted at least 6,276 illegal appointments: 1,498 out-of-panel candidates illegally appointed, 926 involved in rank-jumping, and 4,091 recommended despite OMR score mismatches. CBI forensic analysis of hard disks recovered from an ex-NYSA employee in Ghaziabad revealed manipulation in 952 Class IX-X candidates, 907 Class XI-XII candidates, 3,481 Group C candidates, and 2,823 Group D candidates. The Board issued 2,355 excess appointment letters beyond WBSSC's own recommendations.

F-01

WBSSC destroyed physical OMR sheets (July 2019) despite ongoing recruitment process, violating applicable rules for non-teaching Groups C & D where no destruction provision existed.

F-02

CBI forensic comparison of hard disks seized from ex-NYSA employee Pankaj Bansal (Ghaziabad) with WBSSC server data revealed systematic score inflation across all four categories.

F-03

Board issued 2,355 excess appointment letters beyond WBSSC recommendations. Candidates with blank OMR sheets received appointment letters and joined schools.

F-04

WBSSC applied for supernumerary posts to retroactively accommodate illegally-appointed candidates — a State-level attempt to institutionally launder the fraud.

F-05

Court held delay and laches do not apply as fraud was concealed until 2021–22. The cover-up itself made segregation of tainted/untainted candidates impossible.

F-06

Tainted candidates ordered to refund all salaries + 12% p.a. interest. Untainted candidates' services terminated; entitled to participate in fresh selection with age relaxation.

SC Judgment — Guiding Principle on En Bloc Cancellation

"When an in-depth factual inquiry reveals systemic irregularities, such as malaise or fraud, that undermine the integrity of the entire selection process, the result should be cancelled in its entirety… The decision to cancel the selection en masse must be based on the satisfaction derived from sufficient material collected through a fair and thorough investigation. It is not necessary for the material collected to conclusively prove malpractice beyond a reasonable doubt. The standard of evidence should be reasonable certainty of systemic malaise. The probability test is applicable."
2025 INSC 437, Para 19 — Supreme Court of India, 03 April 2025

SC Judgment: Abhishek Banerjee & Anr. vs. Directorate of Enforcement

Criminal Appeal Nos. 2221-2222 of 2023 · Decided: 09 September 2024 · Bench: Justice Bela M. Trivedi & Justice Satish Chandra Sharma

Case Background

The case arose from the Coal Theft / Money Laundering matter (ED ECIR No. 17/HIU/2020). Inspector Ashok Kumar Mishra allegedly received ₹168 crores in 109 days from coal mafia operative Anup Majee for delivery to "political bosses" including co-accused Vinay Mishra. Funds transferred via vouchers to Delhi and overseas. The challenge was to ED summons under Section 50 PMLA requiring personal appearance in New Delhi.

Key Legal Issues & SC Holdings

PMLA vs. Cr.P.C. Hierarchy
PMLA is a self-contained code; Section 71 gives overriding effect over inconsistent laws including Cr.P.C.; Section 65 applies Cr.P.C. only where not inconsistent.
Section 50 vs. Section 160/161 Cr.P.C.
Not applicable: Chapter XII Cr.P.C. governs "investigation" by police; Section 50 PMLA governs "inquiry" by ED authorities (not police officers). Glaring inconsistencies exist in admissibility of statements.
Constitutional Challenge Art. 20(3), 21
Rejected. Vijay Madanlal precedent holds summons under Section 50 is for evidence collection, not prosecution. Article 20(3) applies only post-formal arrest.
Territorial Jurisdiction
No illegality: HIU not restricted to territorial jurisdiction; alleged transfer of ₹168 crores to Delhi establishes territorial nexus; Abhishek Banerjee has Delhi residence as MP.
Gender-Based Protection
Section 50 is gender-neutral; legislature has expressly carved exceptions elsewhere (Section 45 proviso); courts cannot read in exceptions where none exist.
Complaint under Section 174 IPC
No opinion expressed on merits (pending before CMM); no illegality found in summons or lower court orders; complaint to proceed in accordance with law.
"Proceedings under Section 50 PMLA are 'inquiries' for evidence collection regarding proceeds of crime, distinct from Cr.P.C. Chapter XII 'investigations' for prosecution. Statements recorded under Section 50 PMLA are deemed judicial proceedings (Section 50(4)) and admissible; not hit by Article 20(3) at summons stage."
— Key Legal Principle, SC Judgment 09-Sep-2024

Section 5: Practical Implications & Takeaways

For Legal Practitioners
  • Summons Compliance: Persons summoned under Section 50 PMLA must attend personally or via authorised agent; territorial challenges require demonstrating absence of nexus
  • Document Production: Use Form V compliance; objections to procedure must be raised promptly, not via non-appearance
  • Constitutional Challenges: Article 20(3) claims unavailable at summons stage; may arise only post-formal arrest
  • Gender-Based Relief: No automatic exemption for women under PMLA; seek relief via judicial discretion, not statutory interpretation
For Investigating Agencies
  • Territorial Strategy: Establish clear nexus (fund flows, accused residence) before summoning to non-zonal offices
  • Procedural Rigor: Strictly follow PMLA Rules, 2005 (Form V) to withstand judicial scrutiny
  • Documentation: Maintain records demonstrating inquiry (not prosecution) purpose at summons stage
  • Evidence Preservation: The WBSSC OMR destruction precedent underscores the criticality of contemporaneous evidence safeguarding
For Policy Observers
  • Federal Balance: SC judgment reinforces central agency autonomy in money-laundering inquiries, potentially intensifying state-center tensions
  • Trial Delays: Persistent procedural failures (missing records, witness issues) across Saradha/Narada/SSC cases highlight systemic challenges in complex financial crime adjudication
  • Victim-Centric Gaps: While investigative powers are strengthened, victim/witness protection mechanisms require parallel reinforcement
  • Institutional Design: SSC scam reveals how politicised recruitment bodies create systemic failure cascades affecting tens of thousands of legitimate candidates
